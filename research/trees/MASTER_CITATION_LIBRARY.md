### Source 10: Inquisition Post Mortem (Margaret de Bohun, 1391)
**Archive**: The National Archives, Kew, London
**Citation**: National Archives Reference C 136/75/3
**Evidence**: A contemporary legal record confirming Margaret as the daughter of Humphrey de Bohun and granddaughter of King Edward I
**Significance**: **PRIMARY ROYAL RECORD** - Contemporary government document proving royal descent

**GPS Value**:
- **PRIMARY SOURCE** (created 1391, within Margaret's lifetime)
- **GOVERNMENT RECORD** (official Crown legal document)
- **CONTEMPORARY EVIDENCE** (not compiled centuries later)
- **VERIFIABLE LOCATION** (TNA Kew, accessible to researchers)

**What is an Inquisition Post Mortem (IPM)?**
- Legal inquiry conducted after death of Crown tenant-in-chief
- Established who inherited lands held from the King
- Verified heir's identity and relationship to deceased
- Created by royal officials (Escheators)
- Preserved in official Crown records (Chancery)

**What this specific IPM proves**:
1. Margaret de Bohun died 1391 holding lands from the Crown
2. She was daughter of Humphrey de Bohun, Earl of Hereford
3. Humphrey's wife was Princess Elizabeth, daughter of King Edward I
4. Therefore Margaret was granddaughter of Edward I
5. Her heirs (Courtenay descendants) carry Plantagenet royal blood

**How to Access**:
- Location: The National Archives, Kew, Richmond TW9 4DU
- Reference: C 136/75/3 (Chancery Inquisitions Post Mortem, Series I)
- Catalog: searchable at discovery.nationalarchives.gov.uk
- Viewing: Original medieval document (Latin, vellum)
- Digital: TNA digitization project (some IPMs available online)
- Published calendar: *Calendar of Inquisitions Post Mortem* (HMSO)

**Research visit procedure**:
1. Register for TNA reader's card (free, requires ID)
2. Order document via online catalog (advance notice recommended)
3. View in reading room (photography allowed)
4. Paleography skills helpful (medieval Latin, abbreviated script)
5. Published calendar provides English summary
