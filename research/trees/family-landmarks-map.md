# Family Landmarks Map: Physical Sites of the Brown-Joyce-Vincent-Tregeagle Legacy
## A Geographic Guide to American Family History

This document maps the physical locations—buildings, cemeteries, residences, and landmarks—that define the family's presence in North America. These sites span from the mining camps of California to the collegiate architecture of Utah, representing three generations of immigrant builders, miners, clergy, and craftsmen.

---

## Purpose of This Map

**Historical Geography**: Understanding where your ancestors lived and worked transforms abstract genealogy into concrete history.

**Site Visits**: This guide enables descendants to visit and photograph the physical legacy.

**Preservation Awareness**: Many of these sites are protected as historical landmarks or are in family cemeteries requiring ongoing care.

**Educational Tool**: Demonstrates the geographic breadth of the family's impact on the American West.

## I. The Brown-Joyce Legacy: The Pioneer Mining Foothold

### Primary Sites

**1. Boulder Street, Nevada City, California (Gold Flat District)**
- **Coordinates**: 39.2627° N, 121.0188° W
- **Significance**: Home of Michael Brown and Bridget Joyce from late 1850s through 1888
- **Historical Context**: Gold Flat was a heavily Irish mining community; Boulder Street ran through the center of active placer and hydraulic mining operations
- **Current Status**: Residential area; original wooden structures mostly replaced but street pattern remains
- **What You Can See**: Historic Nevada City downtown (5-minute walk); Nevada County Historical Society Museum (documents Irish mining community); St. Canice Catholic Church (family parish)
- **Cemetery**: St. Canice Cemetery, Nevada City (John F. Brown, Michael's son, remained in area and is buried here)

**2. Holy Cross Catholic Cemetery, Colma, California**
- **Coordinates**: 37.6749° N, 122.4578° W
- **Section**: H, Row 11, Graves 18-22 (perpetual care plot)
- **Interred Family Members**:
  - Bridget Joyce Brown (1835-1912) - Matriarch
  - Thomas J. Brown (1860-1951) - Son, returned from Fresno for burial
  - Margaret Brown (1869-1944) - Daughter
  - Michael Brown Jr. (1866-1937) - Son
  - Annie Brown (1875-1941) - Daughter
- **Historical Context**: When San Francisco banned burials in 1900, Colma became the "City of the Silent" for Bay Area families; Holy Cross served the Irish Catholic community
- **Access**: Publicly accessible; office hours for plot maps
- **What You Can See**: Family plot with multiple Brown/Joyce headstones; perpetual care maintains the site

**3. 3474 18th Street, San Francisco, California (Mission District)**
- **Coordinates**: 37.7618° N, 122.4217° W
- **Significance**: Bridget Joyce Brown's final residence (documented in 1912 obituary)
- **Historical Context**: The Mission District was the heart of San Francisco's Irish immigrant community; Mission Dolores Church (where Bridget's funeral mass was held) is two blocks away
- **Current Status**: Private residence; exterior viewable from street
- **What You Can See**: Victorian-era residential architecture typical of Irish working-class families who achieved stability; Mission Dolores (oldest building in SF, founded 1776)

**4. Santa Rosa, California (Sonoma County)**
- **Coordinates**: 38.4404° N, 122.7141° W
- **Address**: 1007 Santa Rosa Avenue
- **Significance**: Home of Catherine "Kate" Brown Kelly (1858-1934) and her husband John J. Kelly (blacksmith)
- **Historical Context**: Santa Rosa was a thriving agricultural center; the Kelly family represented Irish integration into North Bay trades
- **Current Status**: Address exists; likely modernized from original structure
- **What You Can See**: Santa Rosa downtown historic district; Odd Fellows Cemetery (possible Kelly family burial site)

**5. Fresno, California (Central Valley)**
- **Coordinates**: 36.7378° N, 119.7871° W
- **Address**: East Belmont Avenue area
- **Significance**: Home of Thomas J. Brown and his sister Margaret for 40+ years (1905-1951)
- **Historical Context**: Fresno's agricultural boom drew many Nevada City mining families when the gold played out
- **Current Status**: Residential/agricultural area
- **What You Can See**: Fresno County Historical Museum; agricultural landscape that drew the Brown family south

## II. The Vincent Legacy: The Missionary Trail

### The "Two-Year Foundation" Churches

**1. Church of the Good Shepherd, Lake Charles, Louisiana**
- **Coordinates**: 30.2266° N, 93.2174° W
- **Founded**: c. 1885-1886
- **Rev. Frederick J. Vincent's Role**: Missionary priest who organized first permanent vestry
- **Historical Context**: Lake Charles was a timber and rail hub; Episcopal mission served non-Catholic workers
- **Current Status**: Parish still active (though building may have been replaced)
- **Access**: Episcopal Diocese of Louisiana (https://www.edola.org/)
- **What You Can See**: Modern church building; parish records may include Vincent's name in founding documents

**2. Mission Chapels, Hudson Valley, New York**
- **Coordinates**: ~41.7004° N, 73.9210° W (approximate, specific parishes TBD)
- **Founded**: c. 1887-1889
- **Rev. Frederick J. Vincent's Role**: Missionary-at-Large founding mission chapels in industrial towns
- **Historical Context**: Hudson Valley was experiencing industrial boom; missions served factory workers
- **Current Status**: Many small missions consolidated into larger parishes over time
- **Access**: Episcopal Diocese of New York archives
- **What You Can See**: Research needed to identify specific surviving structures

**3. Good Shepherd Mission, Wichita Falls, Texas**
- **Coordinates**: 33.9137° N, 98.4934° W
- **Founded**: c. 1893-1894
- **Rev. Frederick J. Vincent's Role**: Organized Missionary Guild and secured first building lot
- **Historical Context**: Wichita Falls was a "railroad town" growing rapidly in the 1890s
- **Current Status**: Parish evolved into larger church (now St. Stephen's Episcopal)
- **Access**: Episcopal Diocese of Northwest Texas
- **What You Can See**: Modern church building; parish history may reference Vincent's founding work

**4. St. Peter's Episcopal Church, Salt Lake City, Utah**
- **Coordinates**: 40.7608° N, 111.8910° W
- **Original Location**: 4th South and 2nd West (founding location)
- **Founded/Dedicated**: November 1896
- **Rev. Frederick J. Vincent's Role**: Priest-in-Charge who oversaw construction and dedication
- **Historical Context**: St. Peter's was specifically founded to serve the "Gentile" (non-Mormon) population; Frederick's sons Earl and Paul were born during this ministry
- **Current Status**: Parish still active; moved to new location (20 South and State Street) in 1969
- **Access**: Parish office (801) 359-8714; Episcopal Diocese of Utah
- **What You Can See**: Modern church building; historical records and founding charter with Vincent's signature likely preserved
- **Key Document**: 1896 Parish Charter signed by Rev. F. J. Vincent

**5. Trinity+St. Peter's Episcopal Church, San Francisco, California**
- **Coordinates**: 37.7861° N, 122.4242° W
- **Address**: 1620 Gough Street (at Bush Street)
- **Founded**: 1849 (Trinity); merged with St. Peter's in 2012
- **Rev. Frederick J. Vincent's Role**: Assisting/officiating priest (1899-1920)
- **Historical Context**: Trinity is the "Mother Church of the Pacific"; massive Norman-style building survived 1906 earthquake
- **Current Status**: Active parish (Trinity+St. Peter's)
- **Access**: Public; services welcome visitors
- **What You Can See**: Historic 1892 building (National Register); parish records include Vincent's service; beautiful stained glass and carved wood interior

### The Vincent Family Residences

**6. 1222 Oak Street, San Francisco, California**
- **Coordinates**: 37.7735° N, 122.4347° W
- **Significance**: Vincent family home (1900-1920); documented in 1900 Census as household of Rev. Frederick J. Vincent
- **Historical Context**: Western Addition/Panhandle area; Victorian-era residential neighborhood
- **Current Status**: Private residence (likely modernized)
- **What You Can See**: Exterior of Victorian home; Golden Gate Park Panhandle (2 blocks away); Alamo Square Painted Ladies (similar architectural period)
- **Census Evidence**: 1900 U.S. Federal Census, ED 154, Sheet 4 lists entire family at this address

**7. 1448 Haight Street, San Francisco, California**
- **Coordinates**: 37.7698° N, 122.4459° W
- **Significance**: Vincent family residence circa 1910
- **Historical Context**: Haight-Ashbury district; near Golden Gate Park
- **Current Status**: Private residence
- **What You Can See**: Haight Street Victorian architecture; Buena Vista Park

## III. The Tregeagle Legacy: The Master Mason's Mark

### Signature Works

**1. Karl G. Maeser Memorial Building, Brigham Young University, Provo, Utah**
- **Coordinates**: 40.2518° N, 111.6493° W
- **Address**: BYU Campus, 700 N University Avenue
- **Built**: 1909-1911
- **James Eli Tregeagle's Role**: Lead stonemason and contractor (Belmont Stone Company)
- **Architectural Style**: Greek Revival/Neoclassical
- **Materials**: Sanpete White Oolitic Limestone (hand-carved)
- **NRHP Status**: Listed 1971 (#71000854)
- **Current Status**: Active academic/administrative building
- **Access**: Campus is publicly accessible; building interior accessible during business hours
- **What You Can See**:
  - Four massive hand-carved Ionic columns (Tregeagle's signature work)
  - Intricate dentil molding beneath roofline
  - Carved limestone window surrounds
  - Ashlar masonry showing expert joinery
  - Building nameplate acknowledging National Register status
- **Visit Information**: Free parking available; self-guided campus tours; photography allowed
- **Key Features**: Place your hand on the Ionic column capitals—you're touching stone James Eli carved over 110 years ago

**2. The Tregeagle Residence, Provo, Utah**
- **Coordinates**: 40.2332° N, 111.6560° W
- **Address**: 186 West, First South, Provo
- **Built**: c. 1900-1910
- **Significance**: James Eli's personal residence and "showcase property" demonstrating his stonework capabilities
- **Features (historical): Stone bungalow with decorative copings, balustrades, and stone bridges
- **Current Status**: Private residence (may have been modified or replaced)
- **What You Can See**: Historic neighborhood with early 20th-century homes; area where Belmont Stone Company operated

**3. Provo City Cemetery, Provo, Utah**
- **Coordinates**: 40.2419° N, 111.6402° W
- **Significance**: James Eli Tregeagle burial site (1863-1934)
- **Historical Context**: Many monuments in this cemetery likely carved by Belmont Stone Company
- **Access**: Publicly accessible
- **What You Can See**: James Eli's grave marker (stone, possibly his own work); other Tregeagle family plots

**4. Provo Historic Sidewalks and Curbing**
- **Location**: Downtown Provo, various streets (primarily Center Street area)
- **Built**: 1895-1920s
- **Significance**: Belmont Stone Company was primary contractor for early municipal infrastructure
- **Current Status**: Many sections replaced, but some original limestone curbing and sidewalks survive
- **What You Can See**: Occasional sections of original stonework (look for cut limestone curbs with precision joints)

## IV. The Janvrin Legacy: The Maritime Anchors

### North American Holdings

**1. Arichat, Cape Breton, Nova Scotia, Canada**
- **Coordinates**: 45.5056° N, 61.0019° W
- **Significance**: North American headquarters for Philip & Francis Janvrin & Co.
- **Historical Context**: Major cod fishing and shipping port; Janvrin family owned wharves, warehouses, and over 600 acres
- **Current Status**: Small fishing village; some historical structures survive
- **Access**: Publicly accessible fishing village
- **What You Can See**:
  - Historical plaques referencing Jersey merchant families
  - Le Noir Forge Museum (documents 18th/19th-century maritime trade)
  - Arichat Harbor (where Janvrin ships docked)
- **Research Note**: Isle Madame Historical Society may have Janvrin business records

**2. Janvrin's Island, Nova Scotia, Canada**
- **Coordinates**: 45.5489° N, 60.9772° W
- **Significance**: Small island named for the Janvrin family's extensive fishing operations
- **Historical Context**: Janvrin family established seasonal fishing stations here in the 1700s-1800s
- **Access**: Visible from Cape Breton coastal roads; private property (no public access to island itself)
- **What You Can See**: Island visible from shore; represents the geographic reach of the Janvrin shipping empire

### Channel Islands Origins

**3. St. Brelade, Jersey, Channel Islands, UK**
- **Coordinates**: 49.1925° N, 2.1936° W
- **Significance**: Ancestral home of the Janvrin family for 300+ years
- **Historical Context**: St. Brelade was home to many Jersey merchant families; Janvrin family were shipowners and seigneurs
- **Current Status**: Active parish; St. Brelade's Church dates to 12th century
- **Access**: Publicly accessible; tourism welcome
- **What You Can See**:
  - St. Brelade's Church and cemetery (Janvrin family graves)
  - Jersey Maritime Museum (documents Channel Islands shipping history)
  - St. Brelade's Bay (where Janvrin ships would have launched)
- **Research**: Jersey Heritage Centre (St. Helier) holds Janvrin Letter Books (L/C/40/B/2)

## V. Convergence Point: The San Francisco Bay Area (1899-1920)

### The "1222 Oak Street Moment"

By 1899, all three family lines (Brown-Joyce, Vincent-Tregeagle, Janvrin) had converged in the San Francisco Bay Area:

**The Irish Miners (Browns)**: Bridget Joyce Brown living at 3474 18th Street (Mission District)
**The Clergy (Vincents)**: Rev. Frederick J. Vincent family at 1222 Oak Street (Western Addition)
**The Stoneworkers (Tregeagles)**: James Eli operating 50 miles south in Provo, with sister Laura Alice (Vincent) in San Francisco

**Historical Significance**:
- Three distinct immigrant journeys (Ireland famine, English professional class, Cornish craftsmen)
- Three distinct economic paths (mining → agriculture, clergy, skilled trades)
- All converge in the Bay Area within 20 years of each other
- Represents completion of westward migration that began in the 1850s

## VI. Sites No Longer Extant

### Lost Landmarks

**1. St. Stephen's Episcopal Church, San Francisco (Original Building)**
- **Original Location**: 858-864 Fulton Street (near Fillmore)
- **Built**: Late 1890s
- **Rev. Frederick J. Vincent's Role**: Officiating priest (1899-1920)
- **Current Status**: Building now serves Russian Orthodox congregation (Holy Virgin Cathedral)
- **What Happened**: Episcopal parish moved/merged; building sold to Orthodox church in 1930s

**2. The Original St. Peter's Building, Salt Lake City**
- **Original Location**: 4th South and 2nd West
- **Built**: 1896 (Frederick J. Vincent oversaw construction)
- **Current Status**: Parish moved to new location in 1969; original site redeveloped
- **Legacy**: Founding documents and parish records preserved

**3. Belmont Stone Company Yard, Provo**
- **Original Location**: Near Fifth South and First West, Provo
- **Active**: c. 1895-1935
- **Current Status**: Site redeveloped for commercial/residential use
- **Legacy**: Products of the company (buildings, monuments, sidewalks) survive throughout Utah

## VIII. Research Archives: Where the Documentation Lives

### Primary Repositories

**1. National Archives (Kew, UK)**
- **Holdings**: T71 Slave Compensation Commission records (Janvrin claims)
- **Access**: In-person research room; some records digitized
- **Website**: nationalarchives.gov.uk

**2. Jersey Heritage Centre (St. Helier, Jersey)**
- **Holdings**: Janvrin Letter Books (L/C/40/B/2), shipping records, parish registers
- **Access**: By appointment; research services available
- **Email**: archives@jerseyheritage.org

**3. BYU Special Collections (Harold B. Lee Library, Provo)**
- **Holdings**: Maeser Building construction photos, architectural drawings, BYU history
- **Access**: Reading room by appointment
- **Website**: lib.byu.edu/collections/special-collections/

**4. Episcopal Diocese Archives**
- **California**: 1055 Taylor Street, San Francisco, CA 94108
- **Utah**: 75 South 200 East, Salt Lake City, UT 84111
- **Holdings**: Parish founding documents, clergy records, convention journals

**5. California State Archives (Sacramento)**
- **Holdings**: Nevada County naturalization records, vital records, census microfilm
- **Access**: Research room open to public
- **Website**: sos.ca.gov/archives/

**6. Nevada County Historical Society**
- **Location**: 161 Nevada City Highway, Nevada City, CA 95959
- **Holdings**: Mining history, Irish community records, photographs
- **Access**: Searle Historical Library (research by appointment)
