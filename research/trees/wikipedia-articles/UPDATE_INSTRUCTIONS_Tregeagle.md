## Anticipated Reviewer Questions

### Q: "How do you know the lineage is accurate?"

**A**: The Tregeagle family's presence in Calstock is documented through:
1. Cornwall Record Office parish registers (baptisms, marriages, burials 1680-1898)
2. UK Census records 1841-1881 showing continuous residence
3. Apprenticeship indentures showing formal craft training
4. Protestation Returns 1641 providing 17th-century baseline

These are all primary government/church records, not family tradition.

---

### Q: "Is this relevant to his work at BYU?"

**A**: Yes. The technical skills required for the Maeser Building (precision cutting of Ionic columns, hand-carving of capitals) directly reflect Cornish guild training methods. The article explains HOW he acquired the expertise to execute Neoclassical architecture at monumental scale—through generations of accumulated workshop knowledge.

---

### Q: "Doesn't this make the article too long?"

**A**: The lineage section is ~600-700 words, comparable to sections in other craftsman biographies (e.g., [[Antonio Stradivari]] article discusses multi-generational violin-making tradition). For a master craftsman whose work is on the National Register, documenting craft lineage is standard biographical practice.

---

### Q: "How is this different from just listing ancestors?"

**A**: This isn't genealogy for its own sake—it documents the TRANSMISSION OF CRAFT KNOWLEDGE through documented apprenticeships and guild records. It shows how technical expertise moved across generations via formal training systems, which is historically significant for understanding craft preservation.
