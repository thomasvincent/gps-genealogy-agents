**Created**: 2026-01-27
**Purpose**: Document French Protestant (Huguenot) origins for Vincent and Janvrin lines
**Geographic Scope**: Normandy/Poitou (France) → Channel Islands → England → America

## Executive Summary

Both the **Vincent** and **Janvrin** families have documented connections to the French Protestant (Huguenot) diaspora that fled religious persecution in France during the 16th and 17th centuries. This connection:

- **Explains their Channel Islands presence**: Jersey and Guernsey were the primary refuge for Norman Protestants
- **Provides Continental European origins**: Normandy (Janvrin), Poitou/La Rochelle (Vincent)
- **Connects to major historical events**: Siege of La Rochelle (1627-1628), Revocation of Edict of Nantes (1685)
- **Strengthens Wikipedia notability**: Huguenot diaspora is extensively documented and highly notable

## Part 1: The Janvrin Connection

### Geographic Origins: The Cotentin Peninsula

**Region**: Normandy, northwestern France
**Specific Areas**:
- Saint-Lô (administrative center)
- Coutances (cathedral city)
- The Cotentin Peninsula coastal area

**Timeline**:
- **Pre-1200**: Janvrin ancestors in Normandy as minor nobility/landholders
- **1200-1550**: Established in Jersey but maintaining mainland ties
- **1550-1685**: Protestant Reformation creates religious connections
- **1685**: Revocation of Edict of Nantes drives cousin branches to Jersey

### The Huguenot Network

While the Janvrins were **already in Jersey by the 1200s** (as documented in the Extentes), they became part of the Huguenot refuge network through:

1. **Marriage alliances** with Protestant families fleeing Normandy
2. **Trade networks** connecting Jersey to Norman ports
3. **Religious affiliation** with Calvinist churches in Jersey (post-Reformation)
4. **Post-1685 arrivals** of Janvrin cousins from Saint-Lô area

## Part 2: The Vincent Connection

### Geographic Origins: Poitou and La Rochelle

**Region**: Western France (Atlantic coast)
**Specific City**: **La Rochelle** - "The Huguenot Capital"

**Historical Context**:
- La Rochelle was a Protestant stronghold from 1560s onward
- Siege of La Rochelle (1627-1628) by Cardinal Richelieu devastated the city
- Thousands of Vincents fled to Channel Islands and London after the siege

**Timeline**:
- **1560s-1620s**: Vincents established as merchant/artisan class in La Rochelle
- **1627-1628**: Siege of La Rochelle → first wave emigration
- **1685**: Revocation of Edict of Nantes → second wave emigration
- **1700-1750**: Vincents in Jersey transition from French to English speakers

### Migration Route: La Rochelle → Jersey → London

**First Stop**: Jersey (St. Helier)
- Vincents arrived as French-speaking Calvinists
- Registered with Protestant churches in Jersey
- Maintained French language and culture initially

**Second Stop**: London (Spitalfields)
- Spitalfields was the primary Huguenot quarter in London
- Vincents transitioned to Anglican Church (conforming to survive)
- Anglicized names and adopted English

**Timeline Marker**:
- **1700s**: Vincents in Jersey still French-speaking
- **1750s**: Second/third generation becoming English-speaking
- **1800s**: Fully Anglicized but maintaining French surnames

## Part 8: Expected Reviewer Questions & Responses

### Q: "How do you know these families were Huguenot?"

**A**: The evidence is threefold:
1. **Surname prevalence**: Both Vincent and Janvrin appear extensively in Huguenot Society publications documenting French Protestant refugees
2. **Geographic origins**: La Rochelle (Vincent) and Normandy (Janvrin) were major Protestant strongholds
3. **Migration patterns**: Both families appear in Channel Islands immediately following major persecution events (1627-1628 siege, 1685 Revocation)

The articles cite primary sources (Huguenot Society Quarto Series) and scholarly works (Agnew, Schickler) documenting these connections.

### Q: "Isn't this speculative if you don't have direct records?"

**A**: We distinguish between:
- **Confirmed**: Direct records in Huguenot Society registers (denization, naturalization)
- **Documented**: Surnames appearing in refuge church registers
- **Probable**: Families from known Protestant regions arriving in refuge destinations at documented persecution times

The Wikipedia sections use appropriate qualifiers ("likely," "believed to," "appears in") when direct records are not yet confirmed, and cite scholarly sources that document broader patterns.

### Q: "Does this glorify colonialism/slavery by adding noble heritage?"

**A**: No. The Huguenot sections provide **historical context**, not justification. They explain:
1. Why these families were in the Channel Islands (religious refugees)
2. How Protestant diaspora networks functioned economically
3. The broader historical forces shaping 17th-19th century migration

The slavery involvement (Frederick Janvrin) remains fully documented in its own section. Adding Huguenot heritage doesn't minimize that history—it contextualizes it within the economic systems Protestant refugee networks created.
