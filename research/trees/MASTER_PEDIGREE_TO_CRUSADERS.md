## Phase 3: The Seigneurial Bridge (G7–G12)

### Overview: The Janvrin-Valpy Transition

**Key concept**: The **"dit" name** system in Norman Jersey

**"Dit" means "called" or "alias"**:
- Original surname: **Valpy** (from Old Norse "Valp-r" = young wolf)
- Branch designation: **"dit Janvrin"** = "called Janvrin"
- Over generations, "Janvrin" became the primary surname
- "Valpy" retained as ancestral name in formal documents

**Why this happened**:
- **Norman feudal custom**: Younger sons or cadet branches took "dit" names
- **Land division**: Different branches held different fiefs
- **Legal distinction**: Separate property rights required separate designations

### G14: Johannes Valpy (fl. 1274)

**Flourished**: 1274
**Status**: **Franc-Tenant** (Freeholder)
**Source**: **The Extente of 1274**

**The 1274 Extente**:
- **What it is**: Comprehensive land survey of Channel Islands (Jersey, Guernsey, Alderney, Sark)
- **Purpose**: Document all feudal landholdings after English Crown consolidated control
- **Equivalent**: "Domesday Book" of the Channel Islands
- **Significance**: First comprehensive written record of Jersey feudal system

**Franc-Tenant status**:
- **Not a serf**: Free man, not bound to a lord
- **Land tenure**: Held land directly from the King (as Duke of Normandy)
- **Military obligation**: Provided knight service or paid "chef-rente" (chief rent)
- **Legal rights**: Could sue and be sued in Royal Court, hold property, pass land to heirs

**Why 1274 matters**:
- **English reconsolidation**: After King John lost Normandy (1204), Channel Islands remained English
- **Jersey autonomy**: 1274 Extente confirmed Jersey's special legal status (Norman law under English Crown)
- **Feudal baseline**: This document establishes the Valpy family as established landholders by 1274

**Primary sources**:
- **Extente des Îles de Jersey, Guernesey, Aurigny et Serk, 1274**
  - Original: The National Archives (UK), likely E 36 or C 47 series
  - Published: Société Jersiaise (various editions)

Extente des Îles de Jersey, Guernesey, Aurigny et Serk, 1274. Johannes Valpy
