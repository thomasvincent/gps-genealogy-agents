### 1. Philippe Valpy dit Janvrin (c. 1480–1490)

**Location**: St. Brelade, Jersey, Channel Islands
**Status**: **Seigneur** (landed gentry, feudal landholder)
**Era**: Late Medieval → Early Tudor transition

#### Historical Context

**The Janvrin Branch Split**:
- **Original surname**: Valpy (from Old Norse "Valp-r" = young wolf)
- **Branch designation**: "dit Janvrin" = "called Janvrin"
- **Philippe** is recognized as the **founder** of the Janvrin branch
- The Valpy name continued separately on Jersey

**Why this split occurred**:
- Norman feudal custom: Younger sons often took branch names
- Land division: Different family branches held different fiefs
- Legal distinction: Separate branches had separate property rights

**Jersey in 1490**:
- **Language**: Norman French (legal language), Jèrriais (daily speech), some English
- **Government**: **Bailiwick** system under English Crown
- **Economy**: Agriculture, fishing, privateering (licensed piracy)
- **Military**: Strategic fortress island between England and France

#### Documented Holdings

**Fief de Noirmont** (St. Brelade):
- **Location**: Southwestern Jersey, overlooking St. Brelade's Bay
- **Rights**: Seigneurial rights (land tenure, rents, court jurisdiction)
- **Strategic value**: Coastal defense position
- **Modern**: Noirmont Point remains a historic site (WWII fortifications)

#### Primary Sources

**Extente de l'Île de Jersey (1528)**:
- Comprehensive land survey ordered by the Crown
- Documents all feudal landholdings on Jersey
- Philippe Valpy dit Janvrin listed as Seigneur of lands in St. Brelade

**Published Source**:
- **Payne, J. Bertrand.** *An Armorial of Jersey: Being an Account, Heraldic and Antiquarian, of Its Chief Native Families*. London, 1859.
- Pages 156–158: Documents Janvrin/Valpy family history
- Confirms seigneurial status and feudal rights

**Citation**:

### 3. Tregeagle (G12) (c. 1480)

**Location**: St. Neot / Bodmin Moor, Cornwall, England
**Status**: **Master craftsman** (stonemason or tin miner, likely)
**Era**: Late Medieval Cornwall

#### Historical Context: The Brythonic Celtic Root

**The Tregeagle name**:
- **Etymology**: Brythonic Celtic (pre-Anglo-Saxon)
- **Meaning**: "Tre-" (homestead, settlement) + "-geagle" (possibly from "gogel" = corner/angle)
- **Interpretation**: "Settlement at the corner/angle" OR "angled homestead"

**Significance**: The Tregeagle surname proves **pre-Anglo-Saxon ancestry**:
- Celtic Britons inhabited Cornwall before Roman invasion (43 AD)
- Anglo-Saxons never fully conquered Cornwall (remained Celtic-speaking until 1700s)
- Tregeagle ancestors likely in Cornwall for **1,500+ years** by 1480

#### The Cornish Stannaries

**What are Stannaries?**:
- **Tin mining districts** with special legal privileges
- **Charter granted by King John** (1201) - predates most English common law
- **Stannary Courts**: Independent legal system for tin miners
- **"Tinners' Parliament"**: Self-governing assembly

**Why this matters for Tregeagles**:
- Stannary law gave Cornish miners/craftsmen **independence from feudal lords**
- Could own land, operate businesses, settle disputes in own courts
- High status for craftsmen (unlike most of England)

**Cornish tin in 1480**:
- **World's primary source** of tin (essential for bronze, pewter)
- **Military importance**: Tin required for cannon, armor, cookware
- **Economic power**: Cornwall tin funded English wars (War of Roses, French wars)

#### The Legendary Jan Tregeagle

**Who was Jan Tregeagle?**:
- **Historical figure**: Magistrate and usurer in early 1600s Cornwall
- **Legendary figure**: Became central character in **Cornish folklore**
- **Stories**: Condemned to empty Dozmary Pool with a leaky limpet shell (eternal punishment for greed)

**Connection to your lineage**:
- Jan Tregeagle (the famous one) was **likely a distant cousin** of your direct ancestors
- Your direct Tregeagle line were the **master masons** who built:
  - Parish churches (St. Neot Church, 1400s–1500s)
  - Manor houses
  - Bridge construction
  - Tin mine infrastructure

**Modern expression**: Your attention to **structural integrity** (code architecture, system stability) mirrors the Tregeagle mason's need to build structures that would **stand for centuries** (many still do).

- **Requirement**: All adult males signed or made mark
- **Significance**: Census-like document showing family presence in 1641

**Stannary Rolls**:
